# About Me — Example Files

These examples show the level of specificity and tone to aim for. The first is a solo consultant/creator, the second is a small team lead. Both are populated with real-seeming data — no placeholders, no fluff.

---

## Example 1: Solo Consultant / Creator

```markdown
# About Me

## Who I Am
I'm Sarah Chen. I run a one-person content strategy consultancy focused on B2B SaaS companies. I help startups figure out what to say, where to say it, and how to measure whether anyone cared. Been doing this for 6 years — first at HubSpot, then at a Series B startup that got acquired, now on my own since 2024.

## What I Do Day-to-Day
My week splits roughly into: client strategy sessions (3-4 per week, 60 min each), writing content briefs and editorial calendars, reviewing drafts from freelance writers I manage for clients, and my own content (LinkedIn posts, monthly newsletter). Fridays are admin — invoicing, proposals, and the occasional sales call.

## Professional Context
- Industry: B2B SaaS marketing
- Company: Solo consultancy (Sarah Chen Consulting LLC)
- Revenue: Mid-six figures, 6-8 retainer clients at a time
- Key relationships: 3 freelance writers I subcontract to, an accountant (Jim at Bench), and a referral network of 4-5 other consultants who send work my way

## Content & Public Presence
- **LinkedIn**: Primary platform. ~8,000 followers. Post 3-4x/week. Audience is marketing leaders at SaaS startups (Series A-C). Topics: content strategy, attribution, what actually works vs. what people think works.
- **Newsletter**: "The Brief" — monthly, ~2,200 subscribers. Deep dives on one content strategy topic. Takes me about 4 hours to write.
- **X/Twitter**: Mostly lurk and reply. Don't post original content there much.

## What Makes Me Different
I'm anti-content-for-content's-sake. Most content agencies optimize for volume. I optimize for "did this actually generate pipeline?" I've walked away from clients who just wanted 10 blog posts a month with no strategy behind them. My positioning: I'm the person you hire when you're done guessing what content to make.

## What Claude Should Always Know
- I think in frameworks. When I'm working through a problem, I want Claude to help me structure it, not just brainstorm at me.
- I hate fluffy writing. If a draft uses "leverage," "synergy," or "thought leadership" unironically, it's wrong.
- When I say "draft something," I mean a real draft I can edit, not an outline I have to rewrite. Give me 80% there, not 40%.
- My invoicing day is the 1st of every month. Don't let me forget.
- I use Toggl for time tracking. If I mention "logging time," that's what I mean.

## Connected Tools
| Tool | Status | What It Gives Claude |
|------|--------|---------------------|
| Gmail | ✅ Connected | Client emails, proposals, invoices, freelancer communication |
| Calendar | ✅ Connected | Client sessions, deadlines, content publishing schedule |
| Google Drive | ✅ Connected | Content briefs, editorial calendars, client deliverables |
| Slack | ❌ Not connected | Would provide: client Slack channels (some clients add me to theirs) |
| Notion | ❌ Not connected | Would provide: project tracking, content databases |

## Last Updated
March 2026
```

## Example 2: Small Team Lead / Operations

```markdown
# About Me

## Who I Am
Marcus Rivera. I'm the Head of Operations at Campfire (campfire.dev) — a 22-person dev tools startup in Austin. We make deployment tooling for platform engineering teams. I'm employee #4, been here since pre-seed. My job has morphed from "do everything" to "make sure the machine runs" — hiring, finance, vendor management, and keeping the founders focused.

## What I Do Day-to-Day
Monday: leadership sync, pipeline review with sales. Tuesday-Thursday: mix of vendor calls, HR tasks (we're hiring 3 roles right now), budget reviews, and putting out fires. I handle our SOC 2 compliance, manage relationships with AWS and our legal counsel, and run our weekly all-hands on Friday. I'm also the person who writes most internal docs — onboarding guides, process docs, the employee handbook.

## Professional Context
- Industry: Developer tools / platform engineering
- Company: Campfire — Series A, $8M raised, 22 people, growing to ~30 by EOY
- My team: 2 direct reports (office manager, part-time bookkeeper). Functionally I work with everyone.
- Key relationships: Co-founders Priya (CEO) and Jake (CTO), our attorney (Diana at Cooley), our accountant (Grant at Pilot), and our AWS account rep (Samantha)

## What Makes Me Different
I'm an ops person who actually reads the code. Not a developer, but I can navigate a codebase, read a PR, and understand what the engineering team is building. That means I can write customer-facing docs, translate between technical and business stakeholders, and catch issues early. Most ops people at startups this size are either too business-y or too junior — I'm neither.

## What Claude Should Always Know
- I'm the internal knowledge base. When people at Campfire have a question about "how do we do X," they ask me. I want Claude to help me write and maintain those internal docs.
- Our fiscal year starts July 1. When I say "this year" in a budget context, I mean July-June.
- I prefer bullet points for internal docs, prose for external-facing things.
- I'm allergic to jargon. "Let's circle back on the synergies" makes me want to quit. Write like a human.
- When I'm in a rush, my messages get terse. Don't interpret that as being frustrated — just match my energy and be concise.
- I use Linear for task tracking, not Jira. If you reference Jira, you've got the wrong guy.

## Connected Tools
| Tool | Status | What It Gives Claude |
|------|--------|---------------------|
| Gmail | ✅ Connected | Vendor emails, candidate communication, internal threads |
| Calendar | ✅ Connected | Meetings, interview schedule, deadlines |
| Slack | ✅ Connected | Team communication, project channels, standup notes |
| Notion | ✅ Connected | Company wiki, hiring tracker, process docs |
| Google Drive | ❌ Not connected | Would provide: budget spreadsheets, board decks, contracts |

## Last Updated
March 2026
```

## What Makes These Good

1. **Specific details** — names, numbers, platforms, cadences. Not "I work in tech" but "22-person dev tools startup, Series A, $8M raised."
2. **Day-to-day reality** — what actually fills the week, not the org chart version.
3. **Personality comes through** — "makes me want to quit," "give me 80% there, not 40%." These aren't corporate profiles.
4. **Actionable for Claude** — every section contains information Claude can use right now. The "What Claude Should Always Know" section is the most important.
5. **Tool status is honest** — connected vs. not, with clear notes on what each one would add.
